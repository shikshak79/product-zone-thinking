# Read Your Hand
## Every founder runs five bets. The good ones know which to fold.

---

I built Asellion. Raised twenty million euros. Grew it to one and a half billion in gross merchandise volume, five million in ARR, forty-five people. From the outside, it looked like the playbook was working.

From the inside, every sale depended on me being in the room. I was the scattered founder without a commercial system to match the product. When I wasn't there, deals slowed. When I was there, they closed. The product worked. The way it sold was me.

I didn't have the language for bets. I couldn't frame what I was seeing. In board meetings, I couldn't show my stakeholders why the next expansion wouldn't work. So I agreed to expansions I knew were wrong.

Part of me thought: these people have experience. Maybe I'm drawing the line too conservatively. So I agreed. Again and again. Each new expansion multiplied the workload. Each bet was unique. Out of maybe ten, two worked. Two were passable. Six rejected what we built. Resistance grew. Emotions cooked up.

I went close to burnout. Too many stakeholders, too many open boxes, focus gone. I was surrounded by people who weren't founders. They didn't know the problem from the inside. Nobody talked with me about this.

What I was missing: a way to say, this bet has worked under conditions A, B, C. Here are the thresholds. When we see these signals in six to ten weeks, we proceed. If we don't, we revisit. And here's the next bet after that. A sequence. A way to show what works, what doesn't, and why.

## The pattern

I install commercial buying paths for B2B founders now. Every engagement starts the same way. The founder arrives with what they think is a messaging problem. Within twenty minutes, I've mapped five to seven bets they didn't know they were carrying.

### Five companies inside one

Last week I sat with Nils, a founder in the earth observation space. Eight years of building. Strong product. He listed five expansion directions in twelve minutes: deepen the beachhead, adjacent domain, new geography, B2B2B through partners, horizontal platform. He didn't call them bets. He called them "directions we're exploring."

A bet is a decision with uncertainty, cost, and expected payoff. If you can write it in one line ("If we do X for Y, we expect Z, because signals S"), you have a bet. If you can't, you have a direction. The difference matters because a direction consumes the same energy as a bet while producing a testable outcome of zero.

Nils had five. Each one required its own category frame, its own customer definition, its own commercial path, its own proof. Five companies operating inside one.

### The same pattern, different rooms

I've seen this at a citizen engagement startup: four people, three bets, each requiring completely different sales motions and market knowledge. At a geo-data venture inside a large corporation: the product had become a Frankenstein serving geographic intelligence, survey storage, cable routing, wind farm management. Different markets, different continents, different use cases. Years of work. Millions spent. Partners smiling politely and pushing back. No organic customers.

The smarter the founders I work with, the worse this gets. They can see possibility in every direction. Capability creates options. Options without commitment create surfaces that contradict each other. The homepage goes generic because nobody wants to exclude opportunity. You promise everything and back up nothing. That way, you cannot sell.

## The cost nobody counts

At that geo-data venture, the team had stacked bets. First we'll build this, then it will work, then we'll enter the new market, then we'll add features. Each bet had its own probability. Nobody multiplied them.

### The multiplication trap

If demand confidence is 60%, distribution access is 60%, and delivery capability is 60%, the actual odds are 0.6 times 0.6 times 0.6. That's 22%. A stack of reasonable-sounding bets becomes a very bad hand.

A development team runs at a million euros a year. Product, marketing, sales, tech, design. If that team runs in the wrong direction, hundreds of thousands disappear. When team members leave frustrated, even the experience walks out the door. This counts for bootstrapping founders who struggle for eight years, like Nils. It counts for corporates that fund ventures and write them off. It counts for the funded startups in between.

### Customers can smell half-commitment

Here's what I learned the hard way: when you don't double down on a strategic bet, your customers can sense it. Full dedication creates credibility. You absorb the client's context. You start speaking their language. They look you in the eye and see that you understand. When you juggle seven bets, that depth vanishes. You switch context but can't switch credibility. The homepage goes generic, the pitch shifts per call, the proof stays thin.

Building is maybe 5% of the effort today. AI compressed build time to near zero. The other 95% is making the buying path work: can the right people find you, understand you, trust you, buy from you, use you, tell others? That's the bet. Everything else is a feature request.

## Reading the hand

I worked with Stefan's team at that citizen engagement startup. Good product, growing, but the home market felt too small. Before we sat down, decisions were driven by gut feeling and a simple impact-versus-feasibility matrix. "Don't you believe I can sell in the US?" is a feelings question. It shuts down analysis.

We changed the conversation by putting each bet on a card and asking four questions.

### Does winning this bet change your story?

The US expansion scored high. Proving the model works beyond the home market changes the fundraising narrative entirely. Deepening the beachhead scored lower on this dimension: same story, more of it. A bet that shifts your trajectory is different from one that pulls a lever on the path you're already on.

### Show me the evidence.

For each bet, three checks. Do buyers actually want this? Can you name three? Can you reach them — not in theory, but can you get ten conversations in two weeks? And can you deliver? Have you done something close enough to know it works?

This is where it gets honest. The US bet had early demand signals: a few interested contacts. Distribution was thin. No owned channel. No conference presence. No partner network. Delivery was partially proven because the architecture could handle it.

Each check gets one of three reads. Proven: named evidence on the table. Signals: some indication, not proof yet. Vibes: theory, hope, "we think."

The bet's credibility equals the weakest leg. The weakest, every time, never the average. Even if demand is strong and delivery is solid, if distribution is all vibes, your odds collapse. A strong product nobody can find is a 24% bet that feels like a 90% bet. I've watched teams spend nine months on bets like that because the product was so good they never questioned whether anyone could find it.

### How fast can reality prove you wrong?

Time to truth, not time to build. The European expansion could generate signal in weeks through existing networks. The US bet would take months.

### What's lost if you're wrong?

A messaging change costs nothing. A US expansion with a regional hire is six figures.

## Laying the cards on the table

We put all bets on a board, side by side. The pattern became obvious within a minute.

### The safe bet nobody wanted

The beachhead had strong evidence on every dimension. Proven demand, proven distribution, proven delivery. The team was, as they put it, fed up of eating oatmeal with hot water every day. The growth ceiling was too low.

The US expansion had a stronger prize but weaker evidence. Demand had early signals. Distribution was thin. Pace was slow.

### Research theater

Three other bets had the same pattern: attractive prize, almost no evidence, months before anyone would know. I call this research theater. It looks like strategy. It sounds like risk reduction. It burns three to six months and teaches nothing actionable. Vibes on evidence plus quarters to learn anything. That combination is a fold, every time.

### What happened in the room

The team's reaction surprised me. What I saw on their faces was relief. All bets on the table, all arguments heard, all feelings captured. The completeness changed the energy.

Something I didn't expect: the exercise clarified more than strategy. Expanding into Italy didn't raise the same enthusiasm as the US. That's a signal. Is this a bet you can imagine actually playing? Will it bring you energy? If the answer is no, the evidence barely matters.

They chose the US. They chose it with eyes open: go to events where customers gather, get first conversations, get market voice before going all in. The other bets went back on the shelf, ready to revisit when the evidence changes.

They left with less guilt, less half-heartedness. They'd been doing five things at 20% intensity each. Now one thing at full commitment, with a clear sequence for what comes next.

## The discipline

Good poker players don't play every hand. They fold most of the time. They wait for the spot where the odds favor them. Then they commit. The amateur plays every hand because folding feels like quitting. The professional knows folding is the strategy.

### My own hand

I run this on my own company. Here's my hand, scored.

My primary bet: install commercial buying paths for European B2B deep tech founders, through warmed introductions from channel partners and founder-to-founder LinkedIn presence. Demand has signals. Emmanuel is producing qualified introductions. ClearSky Vision is live in the pipeline. Every First System founder I sit with shows the pattern within twenty minutes. Distribution has signals: two channel partners active, LinkedIn converting to calls, no owned audience yet. Delivery has the methodology tested in VirGeo and Lyrasense, but zero commercial proof at current v3.2 pricing. First Map sale at €3,000 is the next milestone.

The weakest leg is distribution. I have channel partners producing introductions but no repeatable engine that runs without me pushing it. That makes this a playable bet, not a certain one. Verdict: commit. Full resources. Kill switch: three months of focused effort, no paying client at current pricing, I revisit the bet.

Three bets sit next to it. LinkedIn content authority with Roelof Otten as mentor gets a time-boxed test: four weeks to build the habit, eight weeks to see if it generates inbound. The X-RAY self-serve tool at xray.product.zone gets a test: it's live, the scoring works, but I don't yet know if automated scans convert to conversations. A partner-delivered model, training others to run Sprint modules, is shelved. The prize is trajectory-level. Demand, distribution, and delivery are all vibes. That's research theater.

One bet I folded: productizing bet evaluation as a standalone workshop for accelerators. Big prize. No evidence. Quarters to prove anything. Every dimension reads vibes. Fold.

I've decided I'm willing to fund this for another three months from private means. If traction doesn't come, I'll revisit. That's my kill switch. I check the bet score monthly. I keep success and failure signals in mind daily.

One bet gets full commitment. Two or three get time-boxed tests with a kill switch: no evidence in four weeks, fold. Everything else shelved. The ideas are fine. The timing is wrong or the evidence isn't there.

## Play your hand

Write down the bets you're running. Your bets, not your roadmap.

Can you write each in one line? "If we do X for Y, we expect Z, because signals S." If you can't, you have a direction, not a bet.

Pick the strongest. Do you have distribution evidence? Three people with names who will take a call, not "we think we can reach them." If you don't, stop refining the homepage. Go get conversations.

Can you design a test with truth in under four weeks? If not, shrink the bet until you can.

If you don't have distribution evidence and you don't have a fast path to truth, strategy isn't where you're stuck. You're stuck on signals. Go collect evidence first.
